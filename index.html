Case Overview

The Patient & Situation

  • 📋Post-Op Rotator Cuff Repair3 weeks post-surgery
  • 🏴PROM-Only Protocol ActiveNo active motion permitted
  • ⚠️Patient Attempts Active LiftingDuring PTA-led session
  • ❓What do the PT & PTA do?This is our central question

Rotator cuff: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis

PT Role

What the Physical Therapist Does

  • 🔍Evaluates & DiagnosesInitial post-op assessment; confirms early healing phase
  • 📋Writes the Plan of CarePROM-only with documented restrictions; no active lifting
  • ⚖️Retains Full AccountabilityResponsible for all outcomes — even when care is delegated
  • 🔄Authorizes All ProgressionOnly the PT decides when to advance: PROM → AAROM → AROM

"Even when selected physical therapy interventions are directed to a PTA, the PT remains responsible for the care, documentation, and outcomes related to that intervention." — Pagliarulo, p. 53

The PT documents the plan of care that directs every PTA session

APTA supervision guidance1; Pagliarulo4 (pp. 33, 53). See slide 12 for full references.

PTA Role

What the PTA Does

  • 🤜Executes PROM SessionsGuides passive shoulder movement within the PT's plan
  • 👀Actively Monitors the PatientWatches for symptom changes, precaution violations, requests to advance
  • 🧠Applies Clinical Judgment — Within the PlanCan adjust technique; cannot change the plan independently
  • 📞Reports Back to the PT — Same SessionAny clinically significant change requires immediate communication

"Clinical problem solving includes the ability to adjust, modify, or discontinue an intervention within the plan of care established by the PT based on clinical indications." — Pagliarulo, p. 50

The PTA guides passive ROM within the PT's plan

APTA supervision guidance1; Pagliarulo4 (p. 50). See slide 12 for full references.

PT Guidance

How the PT Guides the PTA Well

  • 📄Share the Surgical DocumentationOperative notes, tear size, repair type, tissue quality
  • ✍️Provide Written Advancement CriteriaPROM → AAROM → AROM: specific, not verbal, not vague
  • 🗣️Educate the Patient — Together"Feeling better ≠ tendon ready" — show what they CAN do now
  • 🔔Agree on an Escalation ProtocolWhat gets reported, how fast, who makes the call — before problems arise

Good PT guidance gives the PTA context to make sound real-time decisions — not just a list of restrictions to enforce.

Mentorship in the moment — context, not just restriction

Rehab Context

Rotator Cuff Repair: Rehab Phases

Our case is at Week 3 — the most vulnerable healing stage.

📍 Our Case

Phase 1 · Weeks 0–6

PROM Only — Tendon Healing

Passive pendulums & ROM. No active motion. Sling use.

Phase 2 · Weeks 6–12

AAROM — Active-Assisted Motion

Pulleys, cane-assisted ROM. PT re-evaluates before advancing.

Phase 3 · Weeks 12–16+

AROM & Strengthening

Active motion begins. Resistance bands, isometrics, progressive loading.

Phase 4 · Month 4–6+

Functional Return

Overhead activity, work/sport return — depends on tear size & repair type.

⚠️ Protocols are often generic — PT must obtain operative notes specifying tear size and repair technique before writing the plan. (PubMed 2021)
Recommendations

What Should Happen in This Case

PTA Responds
  • ✋️Stop the active liftingCalmly redirect to PROM
  • 💬Explain the "why"Feeling better ≠ tendon healed
  • 📞Contact PT — same sessionDocument & report before patient leaves
PT Responds
  • 📄Review operative notesTear size, repair type, tissue quality
  • 🔎Determine if reassessment neededFormal re-eval before plan changes
  • ✍️Provide written advancement criteriaSpecific triggers for AAROM/AROM

Going Forward: Establish a pre-agreed escalation protocol — what gets reported, how fast, who decides. No ambiguity during a session.
